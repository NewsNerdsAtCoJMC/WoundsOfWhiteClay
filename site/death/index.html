Death on the Reservation

From drunk driving accidents and suicides to traditional Lakota culture (and the loss of it), death plays an integral, though often tragic, role in the culture of the Pine Ridge Reservation.

Photos and text by James Wooldridge

Traditional Lakota culture places a high value on life. Hunters only took from the great buffalo herds on which the tribes depended when meat was needed, and no part of the animal was wasted. In battle, to count coup was to touch an enemy with a stick and escape uninjured. This was considered a higher honor than killing the enemy.

But make no mistake - the Oglala Lakota also were known as fearless warriors. There is a legendary Lakota rallying cry popularized by the great Oglala war chief Crazy Horse, one he is said to have used at the Battle of the Little Bighorn: “Today is a good day to die!”

It has been more than a century since the Wasichu, or one who takes the best meat for himself, came from the east, slicing up the great prairies with trails, fences and railroads. But now, 128 years after the white man confined the Oglala people to the Pine Ridge Reservation, death lurks around every corner. Life expectancy for a Pine Ridge male is the lowest in the world. Suicide rates dwarf national averages.

These issues are inseparable from alcoholism, which perpetuates hopelessness and despair. Drunk drivers endanger every vehicle on the narrow roads and Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders affect youth at disproportionately high rates. And much of the alcohol comes from Whiteclay, Nebraska.

Eagle feathers are a spiritual symbol of honor to Lakota Native Americans. Eagles are sacred because they fly close to God, or Tunkasila. In an effort to protect bald eagles, only Native Americans can legally possess eagle feathers in the United States. The feathers of this fan were taken from a dead eagle in Colorado, and obtained through the National Eagle Repository.
Everything has a spirit in Lakota religion, from the trees to the Earth to the four directions. After death, a person must travel the Trail of the Spirits, or the Milky Way, in their journey to the Spirit World.
A buffalo skull rests in a cemetery on the Pine Ridge Reservation. Lakota belief holds that the buffalo came to them from inside the Earth for food. Therefore, the Spirit of the Buffalo and the Spirit of the Earth are the same. Until the horns fall off, the Spirit of the Buffalo stays with the skull. Buffalo are sacred to the Lakota, who used to depend on them for survival. A buffalo was only killed when needed, and almost no part was wasted. Then the white man came, shooting hordes of buffalo for sport. They took only the hides and left the carcasses to rot. Soon the great herds had vanished from the plains. And with them went a large part of the Lakota way of life.
On Dec. 29, 1890, Chief Big Foot's Miniconjous had surrendered to Col. James W. Forsyth and his U.S. 7th Cavalry, and were camped on Wounded Knee Creek. The Miniconjous were surrendering their guns in a pile when a shot was fired. No one knows for sure by whom. The 7th Cavalry opened fire with rifles and four Hotchkiss guns, and within an hour, slaughtered about 300 Native men, women and children. For years the massacre was labeled a battle, and 20 Congressional Medals of Honor were issued to the 7th Cavalry - the most ever awarded for a single battle. This Colt Model 1873 .44–40 Peacemaker was taken by Charles W. Allen, a journalist, after the massacre, and is now at the Museum of the Fur Trade in Chadron, Neb., Allen's hometown.
After the bloodshed at Wounded Knee, a blizzard swept over the land. Three days later, the bodies were buried in this mass grave, on the hill where Col. Forsyth's Hotchkiss guns had rested. Years later, Black Elk recalled the incident: "When I look back now from this high hill of my old age, I can still see the butchered women and children lying heaped and scattered all along the crooked gulch as plain as when I saw them with eyes still young. And I can see that something else died there in the bloody mud, and was buried in the blizzard. A people's dream died there. It was a beautiful dream."
A 16-year-old girl on the Pine Ridge Reservation wrote this note before dying by suicide, leaving behind her 1-year-old child. Her sister slipped it under the door of her counselor, Yvonne "Tiny" DeCory. The girl was part of Tiny's Sweet Grass Suicide Prevention Program. Tiny says that it is especially important for children on Pine Ridge (where suicide rates dwarf national averages) to have Native counselors. Some Lakota considering suicide see a figure from their culture called the Slender Man or the Black Spirit. A non-native counselor wouldn't understand, but Tiny believes her people. She has seen his shadow on the reservation. Recently, Sweet Grass' grant money was cut, but Tiny continues to fight suicide on her reservation through prayer and donations.
The old Nebraska welcome sign in Whiteclay now rests inside Denny "Junior" Reynold's lot. Residents of the dry Pine Ridge Reservation often cross the border into Whiteclay, Nebraska, to buy alcohol. The town of 12 residents sells about 3.5 million cans of beer annually.
Battered car bodies are piled in lines in Denny "Junior" Reynold's lot in Whiteclay, Nebraska. Junior says that half of the cars in his yard are from drunk-driving accidents.
On Jan. 25, 2010, a drunk driver veered into a head-on collision, killing Randy Skye Kaline, 30, Summer Rose Kaline, 28, and Burt Benjamin Kaline III, 36. Each had two children. Memorials like these are common on the reservation.
