Ruling Expected Thursday on Whiteclay Beer Sales

By Chris Bowling

The fate of four liquor licenses in Whiteclay will remain in limbo for at least another day as the clock ticks toward their Sunday expiration date. That was the message after an hour-long hearing Wednesday before a Lancaster District Court judge.

Judge Andrew Jacobsen listened to arguments on whether the four beer stores should be allowed to stay open while they appeal a Nebraska State Liquor Control Commission decision. The three-member commission voted unanimously on April 19 not to renew the beer stores’ licenses.

Andrew Snyder, the attorney for the four beer store owners, appealed the ruling and also filed a motion to stay the decision, which would allow his clients to remain open throughout their appeal.

In arguing for the stay, Snyder listed what he said were a succession of LCC missteps, among them: They had no legal right to require the long-form application, they violated his clients’ right to due process and they ultimately came to a biased decision. Snyder frequently cited two Nebraska Supreme Court decisions that he said clearly showed the LCC to be “out of bounds."

“It’s our position, judge, that this is real clear,” he said.

A beer truck exits Whiteclay as people sit on the street on June 27, 2016. Between 2005 and 2015, four beer stores in Whiteclay, Nebraska, a town of 12, sold nearly the equivalent of 42 million 12 oz. cans of beer—enough stacked end to end to run the length of the United States. That alcohol was consumed largely by members of the Pine Ridge Reservation, where alcohol is illegal. Photo by Calla Kessler.

Nebraska Assistant Attorney General Milissa Johnson-Wiles, who argued on behalf of the commission, disputed many of Snyder’s contentions. She said the LCC did have a legal right to request the long-form application, citing state law as her basis. She also said another key factor in the decision was the deteriorating public health and safety issues created by the four beer stores.

Whiteclay’s four beer stores have sold around 3.5 million cans of beer annually in a town of seven, largely to members of the Oglala Lakota. Some Lakota, who live on the nearby dry Pine Ridge Reservation, come to Whiteclay for beer and sometimes end up staying on the streets for days or weeks at a time.

The commissioners, she said, believed looking into “the public health and safety of Whiteclay was long overdue and was a major factor.”

After a closing statement from Snyder, the judge said he would take the case under advisement and deliver a decision as early as Thursday.
